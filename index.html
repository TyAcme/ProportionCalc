Constant of Proportionality (p): a unit ratio that relates one variable's value to another variable.

Constant of Proportionality Formula:
p = yx
where y is the dependent variable and x is the independent variable.

Proportional Relationship Equation:
y = px
A proportion describes how one quantity relates to a whole. It's most often shown as a fraction, though the same idea can be written with a colon, as a decimal, or as a percent.

Like any fraction, a proportion has two parts: a numerator on top and a denominator on the bottom.

Picture a cake sliced into 12 pieces. If 5 get eaten (by that same uncle), 7 remain — so the proportion of cake left is:
712
That same relationship could be written as 7:12, though this calculator only works with the fraction form.
Solving a proportion follows a simple routine: set up both fractions with the unknown labeled, place an equals sign between them, cross-multiply to isolate the variable, then solve. Scaling 7/12 up to a denominator of 96 looks like:
712 = x96
12x = 96 × 7 = 672 → x = 56
This calculator handles that cross-multiplication automatically, which helps most with larger or decimal-heavy numbers.
The golden ratio is a special proportion where the whole compared to its larger part matches the larger part compared to the smaller part, producing the famous value of roughly 1.618:
a + ba = ab
It shows up in architecture, geometry, and golden rectangles, and it's connected to the ratio of consecutive Fibonacci numbers.
This chemistry principle, credited to John Dalton, holds that the masses of elements combining in a compound reduce to small whole-number ratios. For sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), the hydrogen-to-oxygen-to-sulfur mass ratio simplifies down from 2/64/32 to:
H / O / S = 1 / 32 / 16
That's useful for figuring out how much of each element a reaction needs.
A directly proportional (flat) tax charges everyone the same percentage of income no matter how much they earn:
$15,000 × 15% = $2,250
$125,000 × 15% = $18,750
Both taxpayers pay a different dollar amount but the same 15% share of income. This differs from a progressive tax, where the rate itself climbs as income rises.
